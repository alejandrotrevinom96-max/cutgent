# Sources — Personal Finance

Education, not advice; the personal layer overrides generic defaults, and
jurisdiction-dependent items must be verified locally.

- **John C. Bogle** — *The Little Book of Common Sense Investing* (2007; rev. 2017).
  Low-cost index investing, the tyranny of compounding costs, stay the course.
- **JL Collins** — *The Simple Path to Wealth* (2016). High saving rate,
  total-market index fund, avoid debt, path to independence.
- **Morgan Housel** — *The Psychology of Money* (2020). Behavior over math;
  reasonable beats rational; room for error; "enough."
- **William J. Bernstein** — *The Four Pillars of Investing*; *The Investor's
  Manifesto*; *If You Can*. Theory, history, psychology, asset allocation.
- **William Bengen (1994)** and the **Trinity Study (1998)** — origins of the ~4%
  safe-withdrawal rule (US historical data; treat as estimate, see caveats).
- **Dave Ramsey** — *The Total Money Makeover* (2003). Debt snowball, baby steps.
  Sound on discipline; the "no credit ever" and ~12% return assumptions are
  widely critiqued.
- **Vicki Robin & Joe Dominguez** — *Your Money or Your Life*. Money, time, and
  "enough" — foundational to FIRE.
- **Bogleheads wiki** — practical low-cost implementation (US-centric; adapt).

## How to weigh them
Treat low cost, broad diversification, and spending less than you earn as
near-universal. Treat the 4% rule, exact emergency-fund size, allocations, and
account types as rules of thumb / jurisdiction-dependent. For large or irreversible
decisions, consult a fee-only fiduciary and a qualified tax professional.
