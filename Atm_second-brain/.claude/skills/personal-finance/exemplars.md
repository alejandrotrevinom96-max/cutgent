# Exemplars — Personal Finance

Canonical strategies, with honest caveats. Education, not advice.

## 1. Bogle / Index investing
Buy the whole market at the lowest possible cost and hold. You can't reliably beat
the market; you can reliably keep costs low. **Pattern:** a two/three-fund
portfolio (total domestic + total international stock + total bond), expense ratios
near zero, rebalanced annually, contributions automated. **Caveat:** "whole
market" funds and ideal account types are jurisdiction/provider-specific.

## 2. The Simple Path to Wealth (Collins)
Avoid debt, save a large fraction of income, invest the surplus in a low-cost
total-market index fund, let it compound until investments cover expenses.
**Caveat:** heavy single-fund, US-centric; diversify/de-risk as needed.

## 3. The 4% rule / safe withdrawal (Bengen; Trinity study)
Historically, withdrawing ~4% of a starting balanced portfolio (inflation-adjusted)
survived 30 years in past US data — implying ~25x annual expenses. $40k/yr →
~$1.0M (25x). **Caveats (important):** based on historical US returns;
sequence-of-returns risk, longer retirements, lower expected future returns, fees,
and taxes can make 4% too high. Many use 3–3.5% or flexible/guardrail withdrawals.
Not a law.

## 4. FIRE (Financial Independence, Retire Early)
A very high saving rate shortens time to independence (grows assets AND lowers the
target). Variants: LeanFIRE, FatFIRE, CoastFIRE, BaristaFIRE. **Caveats:** depends
on a sustained high saving rate, healthcare access (jurisdiction-dependent), and the
same withdrawal-rate risks.

## 5. Debt payoff: Snowball vs Avalanche (incl. Ramsey critique)
**Avalanche (optimal):** pay minimums, attack the **highest interest rate** first;
minimizes total interest. **Snowball (Ramsey):** smallest balance first for
psychological momentum. **Honest take:** avalanche saves more; snowball can win on
adherence. The debt-payoff discipline and emergency fund are sound; the strict "no
credit cards ever" and "12% return" assumptions are contested — 12% overstates
realistic long-run real returns and ignores fees/inflation.

## 6. Locating assets (general principle)
Hold tax-inefficient assets in tax-advantaged accounts and use tax-advantaged space
before taxable, where available. **Caveat:** account types, limits, and tax
treatment are entirely jurisdiction-specific — verify locally.
