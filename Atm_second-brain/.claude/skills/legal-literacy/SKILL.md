# Legal literacy

> **DISCLAIMER — read first.** This pack is *general legal education*, not legal
> advice, and using it creates no attorney-client relationship. Law varies by
> country, state, and contract; it changes over time; outcomes turn on specific
> facts. Nothing here is a legal conclusion you can rely on. For anything
> consequential — signing, suing, being threatened, money or rights at stake —
> **consult a licensed attorney in the relevant jurisdiction.** This pack helps you
> ask better questions and spot issues; it does not answer them with certainty.

Generic info is the floor. **Whenever `personal/` recall returns the user's real
context — their jurisdiction, entity type, the actual agreement text, prior signed
terms — that OVERRIDES the generic info below.** Surface the conflict, follow personal/.

## Procedure

**0. Jurisdiction first, always.** Ask/recall which country and state/province
governs, and whether the contract names a governing-law clause. Flag explicitly that
the answer can change with jurisdiction. Never assume US law.

**1. Classify the question** into one bucket, then apply that branch:
contract · IP (copyright/trademark/patent) · licensing · entity/structure · compliance (terms/privacy).

**2. Contracts — walk these elements, flag what's missing or one-sided:**
- **Parties** — correct legal names/entities; who is actually bound.
- **Scope / deliverables** — what, by when, acceptance criteria.
- **Payment** — amount, schedule, late fees, kill/cancellation fee, expenses.
- **IP: ownership vs license.** The single most important distinction. *Assignment*
  transfers ownership away forever; a *license* grants permission while you keep
  ownership. "Work made for hire" can make the client the author from the start.
  Flag any clause assigning the user's IP or making their work "work for hire."
- **Termination** — how either side exits, notice, what survives, refund of prepaid.
- **Liability / indemnity** — who pays if something goes wrong; uncapped indemnity
  or unlimited liability is dangerous. Look for a liability cap.
- **Warranties / representations** — what each side promises is true.
- **Governing law / venue / dispute resolution** — which law, where, arbitration?
- **Dangerous clauses to flag (refer to a lawyer):** broad IP assignment,
  uncapped indemnity/liability, non-compete, perpetual/irrevocable license,
  auto-renew + auto-escalation, unilateral amendment, confidentiality with no end,
  "moral rights waiver," personal guarantee, exclusivity with no carve-out.

**3. IP — name the right regime:**
- **Copyright** = original creative expression (text, images, music, video, code).
  Arises automatically on fixation; registration adds enforcement/damages rights.
- **Trademark** = brand identifiers (name, logo) used in commerce.
- **Patent** = inventions/processes. Different, slow, expensive — refer out.
- **Work-for-hire / assignment** — clarify who owns commissioned work; default
  ownership rules differ by jurisdiction and by employee-vs-contractor status.
- **Fair use / fair dealing is NARROW and fact-specific** — never treat it as a
  blanket right; it's a defense decided case by case. Don't assert it as safe.
- **License scope** — always pin down: exclusive vs non-exclusive, **territory**,
  **term**, **media/use** (web, broadcast, print, AI training, commercial vs
  editorial), and whether sublicensing/derivatives are allowed.

**4. Content — what releases/clearances are needed before publishing:**
- **Talent/model release** for recognizable people (esp. commercial use).
- **Location/property release** for private property or trademarked spaces.
- **Music/footage/image/font licenses** — confirm the license actually covers your
  use (commercial? the platform? the territory?). Royalty-free ≠ no terms.
- Confirm chain of rights: did the person granting the license actually own it?

## Boundary — flag and refer, never conclude

State the issue and the questions to ask; do **not** assert a legal conclusion as
certain. Mark uncertainty plainly. Escalate to a licensed attorney for anything
consequential: signing, disputes, threats/letters, large money, irreversible rights,
or anything in an unfamiliar jurisdiction. When in doubt, refer out.
