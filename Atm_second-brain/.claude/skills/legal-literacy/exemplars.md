# Legal-literacy Exemplars (borrow the mechanism, not the surface)

These illustrate good *literacy reasoning* — spotting the issue and routing it. None
is legal advice; each ends by referring a consequential call to an attorney.

## 1. The buried IP-assignment trap in a "design contract"
A founder's freelancer agreement says "all work product, including pre-existing
tools and materials, shall be assigned to Client." Read literally, the freelancer
hands over their reusable code/brushes/templates forever. **Mechanism:** assignment
language is sweeping by default; the danger is in *scope*, not the word "assign."
**Borrow:** separate "deliverables" from "background IP," and propose the freelancer
*license* their reusable tools rather than assign them. Flag for a lawyer before signing.

## 2. Licensing-scope mismatch — "royalty-free" music in a paid ad
A creator uses a "royalty-free" track in a client's TV commercial. The license
covered *web, non-broadcast, single project*. The use is broadcast + commercial =
out of scope = infringement exposure. **Mechanism:** royalty-free means "no
per-play royalty," NOT "any use allowed." Scope (media, territory, term,
commercial) is the real boundary. **Borrow:** before using any asset, match the
license's media/use/territory/term against the actual deployment — then keep the
license file as proof.

## 3. The missing talent release on a recognizable face
A brand repurposes user-generated content showing an identifiable customer in a paid
campaign with no release. **Mechanism:** commercial use of a person's likeness
implicates right-of-publicity/privacy independent of copyright — owning the photo
isn't enough. **Borrow:** for any recognizable person in commercial content, get a
signed model/talent release; for minors, a guardian's. When unsure if use is
"commercial," treat it as such and refer out.

## 4. Copyright vs trademark confusion on a logo
A founder asks "should I copyright my brand name?" **Mechanism:** a brand *name* is
protected by trademark (use in commerce), not copyright (creative expression); a
*logo's artwork* can carry both. Naming the right regime changes the entire next
step (USPTO/registry search vs nothing to do). **Borrow:** classify the asset
before advising any filing; don't conflate the regimes.

## 5. Fair use asserted as a safe blanket right
Someone plans to use 30 seconds of a hit song in monetized content "because it's
fair use." **Mechanism:** fair use is a fact-specific *defense* (purpose, nature,
amount, market effect) decided case by case — not a permission you can claim in
advance. **Borrow:** never green-light a use on "it's fair use"; describe it as
uncertain, note the four-factor test exists, and refer the consequential call out
(or license the track).

## 6. Uncapped indemnity hidden in friendly terms
A SaaS contract reads collaboratively but the indemnity clause makes the user liable
for *any* third-party claim "arising from" the engagement, with no cap. **Mechanism:**
liability/indemnity is where catastrophic, asymmetric risk lives; the dollar exposure
is invisible until something goes wrong. **Borrow:** always locate the liability cap
and indemnity scope; flag uncapped/unlimited exposure as a must-review-with-counsel item.
