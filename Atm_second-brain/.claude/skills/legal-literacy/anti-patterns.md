# Legal-literacy Anti-Patterns (detectable failure modes + fix)

## 1. Giving confident legal advice
**Tell:** stating "this clause is fine / unenforceable / you're safe" as fact, with
no hedge and no jurisdiction.
**Fix:** describe the issue and the questions it raises, mark it uncertain, and refer
any consequential decision to a licensed attorney. Literacy, not verdicts.

## 2. Assuming US law universally
**Tell:** citing US doctrines (work-for-hire, DMCA, LLC) to a user whose
jurisdiction is unknown or non-US.
**Fix:** ask for / recall jurisdiction first; state that rules and even terminology
differ by country and state. Don't default to America.

## 3. Treating fair use as a broad right
**Tell:** "you can use it, it's fair use / it's only X seconds / you credited them."
**Fix:** fair use (fair dealing elsewhere) is a narrow, fact-specific *defense*, not
a blanket permission, and credit doesn't cure infringement. License it or refer out.

## 4. Signing away IP unknowingly
**Tell:** skimming past "assignment," "work made for hire," "all rights," or "moral
rights waiver" without flagging that ownership is leaving.
**Fix:** treat any ownership-transfer language as high-priority; distinguish
assignment from license; surface what the user would lose forever before they sign.

## 5. Copy-paste boilerplate without reading
**Tell:** grabbing a template contract/NDA/terms off the internet and using it as-is.
**Fix:** boilerplate often has wrong jurisdiction, wrong party names, mismatched
scope, or one-sided terms; read every clause against the actual deal and have counsel
review anything consequential.

## 6. Confusing the IP regimes
**Tell:** "copyright your business name," "trademark your blog post," "patent your logo."
**Fix:** map asset to regime first — copyright (expression), trademark (brand
identifiers in commerce), patent (inventions). The right regime changes every next step.

## 7. Ignoring license scope
**Tell:** "it's royalty-free / I bought it, so I can use it anywhere."
**Fix:** check media/use, territory, term, exclusivity, commercial-vs-editorial, and
sublicensing against the actual deployment; keep the license file as proof of rights.

## 8. Missing releases and chain-of-rights
**Tell:** publishing content with recognizable people, private locations, or
sub-licensed assets without releases or proof the grantor owned the rights.
**Fix:** get talent/location releases for commercial use; verify the licensor
actually held the rights they granted before relying on the license.
