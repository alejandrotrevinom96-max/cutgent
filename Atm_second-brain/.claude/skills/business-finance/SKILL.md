# Business Finance

A binary, decision-first procedure for a founder/small business. Generic best
practice is the floor. **Whenever `personal/` recall returns the user's real
numbers — bank balances, burn, entity type, jurisdiction, prior advice from their
CPA — that OVERRIDES the generic defaults below.** Surface the conflict, then
follow personal/.

## DISCLAIMER (state to the user)

This is **general business-finance education, not accounting, tax, legal, or
investment advice**, and creates no professional relationship. Treatment depends on
your entity type and jurisdiction. For filings, tax treatment, entity structuring,
or any decision with real money on the line, **consult a CPA or licensed
accountant**. Never assert a tax conclusion as certain — flag it to a professional.

## 1. Cash is king (run this first)

- **Runway = cash on hand ÷ net monthly burn.** Net burn = cash out − cash in per
  month. If burn is negative (cash-flow positive), runway is not the constraint.
  Always know this number; recompute monthly. < 6 months of runway is a red alert.
- **Profit ≠ cash ≠ revenue.** Revenue is what you billed, profit is revenue minus
  expenses, cash is what's actually in the bank. A profitable business can still die
  of a cash crunch (slow receivables, inventory, taxes due). Watch all three.
- **Separate business and personal accounts and cards — always.** Commingling
  breaks bookkeeping, weakens any liability protection, and complicates taxes. This
  is non-negotiable.

## 2. The three statements (what each answers)

- **P&L / income statement** — *Am I profitable over a period?* Revenue − COGS =
  gross profit; − operating expenses = operating income; − interest/tax = net income.
- **Balance sheet** — *What do I own and owe right now?* Assets = Liabilities +
  Equity, at a point in time.
- **Cash-flow statement** — *Where did cash actually move?* Operating, investing,
  financing. Reconciles net income to the change in cash. This is the one founders
  under-read.

## 3. Unit economics (does one sale make money?)

- **Contribution margin** = price − variable cost per unit. If negative, you lose
  money on every sale; growth makes it worse.
- **CAC** = fully-loaded cost to acquire one customer. **LTV** = gross margin ×
  customer lifetime. Healthy rule of thumb: **LTV/CAC ≥ 3** and **CAC payback < ~12
  months** (model/industry dependent — treat as a heuristic, not a law).
- **Breakeven (units)** = fixed costs ÷ contribution margin per unit. Know it.

## 4. Pricing and margin

- **Price to value and to a target margin, not just cost-plus.** Cost sets the
  floor; willingness-to-pay sets the ceiling. Compute the true fully-loaded cost
  (include labor, fees, returns, support) before claiming a margin.
- A price increase usually beats a cost cut for the same margin gain. Test small.

## 5. Bookkeeping discipline

- **Know cash vs accrual basis.** Cash = record when money moves; accrual = record
  when earned/incurred. Accrual shows true profitability; many file taxes on cash
  basis. Which applies is a CPA question.
- **Cadence:** categorize transactions weekly; **reconcile every account monthly**
  against statements. Keep receipts. Close the books monthly so reports are real.
- **Fixed vs variable costs:** know which is which — it drives breakeven, pricing,
  and what you can cut fast in a crunch.

## 6. Budget, forecast, taxes, funding

- **Budget vs actual:** set a budget, compare to actuals monthly, explain variances.
- **Rolling forecast:** maintain a 12-month rolling cash forecast; update it monthly.
  A 13-week cash-flow forecast is the survival tool in a tight period.
- **Set aside for taxes as you earn** (income, self-employment, sales/VAT,
  payroll). Park it in a separate account. Rates/obligations are jurisdiction- and
  entity-specific — confirm with a CPA; do not state a rate as certain.
- **Funding only against a real use-of-funds.** Debt vs equity vs bootstrapping is a
  tradeoff (dilution, control, repayment risk, runway extension). Raise to hit a
  specific milestone with a concrete plan — not "to have a cushion."

margin) from jurisdiction/entity-dependent rules (tax rates, deductibility, basis,
structuring) — flag the latter to a CPA and never assert it as fact.
