# Business-Finance Exemplars (borrow the mechanism, not the surface)

## 1. The 13-week cash-flow forecast — surviving a crunch (turnaround/CFO practice)
A business with shrinking runway stops obsessing over the annual P&L and builds a
week-by-week projection of cash in and cash out for the next 13 weeks: confirmed
receivables by expected date, payroll, rent, tax payments, loan service. The gap
where the balance dips below zero is the action point. **Mechanism:** cash timing,
not period profit, is what kills companies — a weekly horizon makes the failure date
visible early enough to act. **Borrow:** when runway is tight, model cash by week,
not profit by quarter; fix the date of the first negative balance.

## 2. Contribution margin before scaling — the negative-unit trap (unit economics)
A delivery startup grew orders fast while losing money on each order (price below
fully-loaded variable cost). Every new customer deepened the loss; raising volume
accelerated the cash burn. The fix was to make contribution margin positive
(reprice, cut variable cost) *before* spending on growth. **Mechanism:** if
price − variable cost < 0, scale multiplies losses. Growth only helps once the unit
makes money. **Borrow:** prove positive contribution margin first; treat growth
spend as illegitimate until it's positive.

## 3. LTV/CAC and payback — disciplined acquisition spend (SaaS finance)
A subscription business found CAC was fine but payback took 20 months while it only
had 9 months of runway — so acquisition was draining cash faster than it returned.
It capped spend to a CAC payback under runway and chased LTV/CAC ≥ 3. **Mechanism:**
even profitable-on-paper acquisition can be a cash trap if payback is slower than
runway. **Borrow:** compare CAC payback against runway, not just against LTV; the
heuristic ratios are a starting screen, not a verdict.

## 4. Pricing to value, not cost-plus — the margin lever (pricing strategy)
A maker priced handmade goods at cost × 2 and stayed broke. Repricing to perceived
value (and fully loading cost with their own labor, fees, and returns) roughly
doubled margin with little volume loss. **Mechanism:** cost sets the floor,
willingness-to-pay sets the ceiling; cost-plus leaves the gap on the table and often
forgets hidden costs. **Borrow:** load every real cost in, then price to value and a
target margin; test a small increase before assuming demand will collapse.

## 5. Separate accounts + monthly reconciliation — clean books that don't lie
A founder ran everything through one personal card, guessed at profit, and got a tax
surprise. Splitting into a business account/card and reconciling monthly against
statements turned the books into something decisions (and a CPA) could trust.
**Mechanism:** commingled, unreconciled books produce numbers that quietly drift
from reality; separation plus monthly reconciliation keeps them honest. **Borrow:**
separate accounts on day one; reconcile every account monthly before reading any
report.

## 6. Tax set-aside as you earn — the deferred-cash discipline
A consultant treated gross receipts as income, spent it, then owed a large tax bill
with no cash to pay. Moving a fixed percentage of every payment into a separate tax
account as it arrived removed the shock. **Mechanism:** taxes are a real liability
accruing in real time even though they're paid later; treating earned cash as fully
yours is borrowing from a future bill. **Borrow:** sweep a tax reserve on receipt,
in a separate account; confirm the actual rate/obligation with a CPA rather than
guessing.
