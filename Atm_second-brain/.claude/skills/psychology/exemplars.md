# Exemplars — Psychology

Education, not diagnosis. Borrow the *mechanism*, check the evidence, let the
personal layer override.

## Self-Determination Theory for real motivation
Someone keeps quitting a goal pursued only for an external reward. Redesign around
**autonomy** (their own reasons), **competence** (right difficulty, visible
progress), **relatedness** (with/for people). **Why:** intrinsic motivation is more
durable; piling on rewards can undermine interest (overjustification).

## Habit loop done right (not willpower)
Make the **cue** obvious, the **routine** trivially easy, the **reward** immediate —
and shrink it (Tiny Habits) so B=MAP is satisfied even on a low-motivation day,
anchored to an existing routine. To break one, raise friction and remove the cue.
**Why:** behavior tracks ability and prompts far more than motivation.

## Cialdini's principles used ethically
Read why a request landed (reciprocity, social proof, authority, scarcity) and use
one *honestly* — genuine social proof, a real deadline. **Why:** the line is using
true principles to help a real decision, never fabricating them.

## Outside view on a bias
Before "I'm just bad at estimating," take the **base rate**: most people
underestimate task time (planning fallacy). **Why:** biases are systematic;
reference-class data beats introspection. (Decision-making owns this.)

## Replication-aware reading of a famous study
"Power posing changes your hormones and confidence" — largely **failed to
replicate**; at most a small self-report effect. Report it as contested with a
small/uncertain effect, not fact. **Why:** weight replication and effect size over
the headline.

## Reappraisal for an emotion
A setback feels like proof of failure. Name the emotion, then **reappraise** the
appraisal ("this is a data point, not a verdict") rather than suppress it. **Why:**
reappraisal is well-supported and healthier than suppression. Persistent distress ->
a professional.
