# Photography

A binary, decision-first procedure for making a still image on purpose. Generic
craft is the floor. **Whenever `personal/` recall returns the user's real context
— their bodies/lenses, signature look, recurring subjects, prior edits/presets —
that OVERRIDES the generic moves below.** Surface the conflict, follow personal/.

## 0. Intent first

Before settings: what is the *subject*, and the *one idea* about it? A photo with
no subject and no intent can't be fixed in the edit. Decide the feeling, then make
every choice (light, frame, lens, moment) serve it.

## 1. Light first — you photograph light, not things

Read four properties before anything else:
- **Quality:** hard (small/distant source — crisp shadows, drama) vs soft
  (large/close source — gentle gradation, flattering). Bigger/closer = softer.
- **Direction:** front (flat, lowest form), side (reveals texture/shape, most
  three-dimensional), back/rim (separation, mood, flare), top (drama or
  unflattering raccoon-eye).
- **Color/white balance:** match or deliberately contrast warm/cool. Golden and
  blue hour give built-in mood; mixed light needs a WB decision.
- **Ratio:** lit-vs-shadow difference = contrast. Fill (reflector, second source,
  ambient) raises shadows to taste.
Natural: position the subject relative to sun/window. Modified: shape it
(diffuse, bounce, flag, gel). When light is bad, move — or wait.

## 2. Exposure triangle — three controls, three creative trades

Each leg buys correct brightness but *costs a creative variable*. Set the one
the picture cares about first, then balance the rest.
- **Aperture (f-stop):** depth of field. Wide (f/1.4–2.8) isolates the subject,
  blurs background; narrow (f/8–16) holds front-to-back sharpness.
- **Shutter speed:** motion. Fast (1/1000+) freezes; slow (1/30 and below)
  blurs/streaks. Hand-hold rule: 1/(focal length), faster on crop.
- **ISO:** noise. Keep as low as the scene allows; raise only after aperture and
  shutter are committed. A sharp noisy frame beats a clean blurry one.
Use the **histogram**, not rear-LCD brightness. **Expose to the right** (push
tones high without clipping) to maximize data and minimize shadow noise, then pull
down in the edit. Protect highlights you can't recover.

## 3. Composition — simplify, then arrange

The first job is subtraction: remove or hide everything not serving the idea.
Then place:
- **Thirds / balance:** put subject and horizon off-center unless symmetry is the
  point. Balance visual weight.
- **Leading lines** pull the eye in; **frame within a frame** (doors, branches)
  adds depth and focus.
- **Negative space** gives the subject room; clutter kills it.
- **Foreground/background layers** build depth. Check *edges, corners, and
  background* before pressing — mergers and bright distractions ruin frames.

## 4. Lens & perspective — distance is the real control

Focal length changes *how much* and the *rendering*, not how close you are:
- Wide (16–35mm): expansive, exaggerates depth/foreground, distorts faces up
  close. For environment and scale.
- Normal (35–50mm): natural perspective, close to human seeing.
- Tele (85–200mm): compresses planes, flattering portraits, isolates.
**Perspective is set by where you stand, not the lens.** Move your feet to fix
subject/background relationships; choose focal length for framing/compression.

## 5. Moment & anticipation

For anything alive, timing is the photo. Anticipate — pre-focus, pre-frame, read
the gesture/peak before it happens (Cartier-Bresson's decisive moment). Burst when
peak is unpredictable; otherwise wait for the one frame.

## 6. People — pose, direction, flattering angles

- **Flatter:** soft light from the side/45°; lens 85mm-equivalent or longer;
  shoot near eye level or slightly above for faces.
- **Pose:** create angles and space — weight on back foot, chin slightly forward
  and down, shoulders off-square, hands with purpose, gaps between limbs and
  torso. Bend joints; avoid limbs straight to camera.
- **Direct, don't just pose:** give an action or thought; shoot between
  instructions for genuine expression. Watch the eyes and catchlight.

## 7. The edit pipeline (order matters)

1. **Cull ruthlessly.** Reject technical failures and near-duplicates; pick the
   one best frame per moment. Ship fewer, stronger images.
2. **Global before local.** White balance → exposure/contrast/tone curve →
   highlights/shadows → color (HSL, grade) → THEN local (dodge/burn, masks).
3. **Retouch with restraint.** Heal distractions; keep skin and texture real.
4. **Crop** to strengthen composition, not to rescue a missed frame.
5. **Output-sharpen per medium** (screen vs print), export to the target color
   space/size. Sharpen last, for the destination.

## Honesty boundary

In documentary/journalistic contexts, disclose any manipulation beyond global
tone/exposure/crop; never add, remove, or move content. Respect subject consent,
model/property releases, and privacy.
