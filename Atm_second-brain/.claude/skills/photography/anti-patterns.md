# Photography Anti-Patterns (detectable failure modes + fix)

## 1. No subject / no intent
**Tell:** the frame "is nice" but you can't say what it's of or what it's about;
the eye wanders with nowhere to land.
**Fix:** decide the subject and the one idea before shooting. If you can't name
them, you have nothing to compose, light, or expose *for*.

## 2. Cluttered frame
**Tell:** competing elements, busy background, distractions at the edges; the
subject fights the surroundings.
**Fix:** subtract. Change angle/distance, use a wider aperture to blur, or fill
the frame with the subject. Simplify before you arrange.

## 3. Flat front-lighting
**Tell:** on-axis (built-in flash / sun behind you) light flattens form, kills
texture, and gives lifeless skin and red-eye.
**Fix:** move the light off-axis — side or 45° — or move the subject relative to
the window/sun. Direction is what creates three-dimensionality.

## 4. Blown highlights / crushed shadows
**Tell:** white skies and faces with no detail; pure-black shadows with no
information; you trusted the rear LCD.
**Fix:** read the histogram, expose to protect recoverable highlights, add fill
or bracket/HDR-merge when dynamic range exceeds the sensor. Recover in raw.

## 5. Over-editing / HDR halos / plastic skin
**Tell:** glowing halos around edges, gray flat midtones, oversaturated color,
waxy texture-less faces, crunchy over-sharpening.
**Fix:** edit globally first and with restraint; keep skin texture; pull
saturation back; sharpen for the medium, not to taste. Less, later, local.

## 6. Centered-everything / dead-center subject
**Tell:** every shot has the subject and horizon nailed to the middle by default.
**Fix:** use thirds and balance; place horizon high or low; offset the subject —
or commit to symmetry deliberately when it serves the idea, not as a reflex.

## 7. Wrong tool for depth of field / motion
**Tell:** group shot at f/1.4 with half the faces soft; action frame at 1/60
smeared; portrait at f/16 with a distracting sharp background.
**Fix:** choose aperture for how much must be in focus and shutter for the motion
intent *first*, then let ISO float. Match the setting to the picture's need.

## 8. Lens distortion / wrong working distance for faces
**Tell:** portraits shot with a wide lens up close — big noses, bulging foreheads,
warped edges.
**Fix:** step back and use a longer focal length (85mm-equivalent+) for flattering
compression; reserve wide lenses for environment and scale, not tight face shots.
