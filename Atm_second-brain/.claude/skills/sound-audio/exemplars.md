# Sound & Audio Exemplars (borrow the mechanism, not the surface)

## 1. Star Wars lightsaber & Wilhelm — sound design from real-world sources (Ben Burtt)
Burtt built the lightsaber hum by recording a worn film-projector motor plus a TV
tube buzz picked up on a mic, then adding movement by waving a speaker past a mic
for Doppler. **Mechanism:** memorable sound design is layered real recordings
shaped to picture, not synthetic presets. **Borrow:** when designing an effect,
record or layer organic source material and process it; design intentionally to
support the moment rather than dropping in a stock hit.

## 2. Film dialogue mixing — words-first, music ducks (any well-mixed feature; e.g. *Dunkirk* vs. its critics)
Christopher Nolan films are famously argued over precisely because intelligibility
is sacrificed for impact — the counter-example proves the rule. Conventional craft
keeps dialogue clearly on top and rides/ducks score and effects beneath it.
**Mechanism:** the human ear locks to voice; everything else is a bed.
**Borrow:** carve an EQ pocket for the voice, duck music several dB under it, and
verify on a phone speaker, not just the dub stage.

## 3. EBU R128 / loudness normalization — deliver to a number, not to a vibe (ITU-R BS.1770)
Broadcast adopted -23 LUFS integrated measurement (BS.1770 algorithm) to end the
era of jarring commercial loudness jumps. Streaming followed with normalization
around -14 LUFS. **Mechanism:** loudness is a measured, agreed target with a
true-peak ceiling — playback systems normalize anyway, so over-loud masters just
get turned down and lose dynamics. **Borrow:** meter to the platform's LUFS spec
and stop "mastering louder."

## 4. The Loudness War & "Death Magnetic" — what over-limiting destroys (Metallica, 2008)
The CD master was crushed so hard it clipped audibly; fans preferred the quieter,
more dynamic Guitar Hero stems. **Mechanism:** slamming a limiter for maximum
loudness flattens transients, adds distortion, and fatigues the listener — and
normalization erases the loudness "win" anyway. **Borrow:** leave dynamic range;
let the limiter catch peaks, not pulverize the whole mix.

## 5. Glyn Johns / classic tracking — capture is the mix (Led Zeppelin, The Who)
Glyn Johns got finished-sounding drums with a handful of well-placed mics in a
good-sounding room, minimal processing. **Mechanism:** mic choice, placement, and
room do the heavy lifting; the "mix" is largely decided before any fader moves.
**Borrow:** invest time moving the mic and treating the space; a great source needs
little repair, a bad one can't be fully saved.

## 6. Podcast/voiceover chain — subtractive-first, gentle compression (broadcast voice practice)
Pro spoken-word chains high-pass the rumble, cut boxiness/harshness before any
boost, compress a few dB for consistency, de-ess, then limit to ~-16 LUFS.
**Mechanism:** an ordered, restrained chain yields an intelligible, even voice that
survives phone and earbud playback. **Borrow:** follow the chain order, cut before
you boost, and target the spoken-word loudness spec rather than maxing level.
