# Decision-making

Choose well when the outcome is uncertain. Judge a decision by its *process and
information at the time*, not by how it turned out (Duke's "resulting" is the error).
A good process can yield a bad outcome and vice versa; you control the process.

## Personal layer overrides this
Recall the user's `personal/` context: goals/values, real risk tolerance and capacity
for loss, constraints, and how past similar calls went. **Personal context wins** —
the right risk level is theirs, not the textbook's.

## The method
1. **Frame the actual decision.** Write the real question and options. State *what
   would change your mind* — the evidence that would flip you.
2. **Match effort to reversibility.** Two-way door (reversible): decide fast and
   move. One-way door (irreversible/expensive to undo): slow down, deliberate. Most
   decisions are two-way doors treated as one-way.
3. **Take the outside view (base rates).** How do cases like this usually turn out?
   Anchor on the reference class, then adjust. Inside-view-only is overconfident.
4. **Generate real alternatives.** Avoid the "whether or not" trap. Force >=3
   genuinely different options (including doing nothing and a reversible test).
5. **Estimate expected value and downside separately.** Rough probabilities x
   payoffs for upside; then name the downside and whether you can survive it. A
   positive-EV bet you can't survive the tail of is still a no.
6. **Premortem + inversion.** Imagine it's a year later and it failed badly — write
   why (Klein). Invert (Munger): ask "how would this surely fail," then avoid those.
7. **Check predictable biases.** Confirmation (only seeking support?), sunk cost
   (deciding on past spend?), anchoring (one early number framing all?),
   overconfidence (would I bet on my forecast?).
8. **Decide and document.** Make the call proportional to the door; write the
   decision, key assumptions, expectations, and what would change your mind.
9. **Review calibration later.** At the outcome, judge the process regardless of
   result; were your probabilities calibrated? Update incrementally (Tetlock).

## Defaults
- Two-way door? Stop deliberating and run a cheap reversible test.
- When confident, argue the opposite case before committing.
- "We've already invested so much" is a sunk-cost flag, not a reason.
- A forecast without a number and a confidence is a vibe; attach both.
