# Marketing & Growth — Exemplars

## April Dunford — positioning (*Obviously Awesome*)
Five components in order: competitive alternatives (often a spreadsheet or doing
nothing) -> unique attributes -> value -> target customers who care most -> market
category. **Why:** derives the message from the customer's real alternatives, so the
claim is differentiated, relevant, AND provable. Anti-example: "the leading
all-in-one platform" — names no alternative, no unique attribute, no segment.

## Sean Ellis & Morgan Brown — *Hacking Growth*
North-star metric (Airbnb nights booked, not signups) + high-tempo testing across
AARRR. **Why:** growth becomes a process of validated learning, not hero launches.
Activation ("aha") is the first lever, not acquisition.

## Brian Balfour — channel/model fit (Four Fits)
Market -> Product -> Channel -> Model must all fit. A channel must fit your price
and margin. **Why:** explains the most common failure — copying a channel that
worked for different economics. Anti-example: a $15/mo app hiring AEs for outbound.

## Weinberg & Mares — *Traction* (Bullseye)
Brainstorm every channel (outer ring) -> rank the few promising (middle) -> test
cheaply in parallel (inner) -> focus on the one that works. **Why:** breadth in
consideration, discipline in commitment.

## Donald Miller — *Building a StoryBrand*
Customer is the hero, brand is the guide: a character with a problem meets a guide
who gives a plan and a call to action. **Why:** clarity beats cleverness; buyers act
on a message they grasp in seconds. Anti-example: "We are passionate about
innovation" makes the company the hero.

## Honesty as growth
Overclaiming spikes acquisition but degrades activation, retention, and referral —
three of five AARRR stages. The compounding asset is trust.
