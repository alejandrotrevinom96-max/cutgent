# Career

A binary procedure for direction, positioning, and moves. Generic best practice is
the floor. **Whenever `personal/` recall returns the user's real context — their
goals, values, history, constraints, prior offers and wins — that OVERRIDES the
generic moves below.** Surface the conflict, then follow personal/. Comp *mechanics*
(anchoring, concessions, BATNA) belong to the negotiation pack — this pack decides
*what* to ask for and *whether* to take a role; negotiation runs the exchange.

## 1. Direction — the overlap, not the title

Find the intersection of three sets, concretely (not as a vibe):
- **What you're good at** — demonstrated strengths, not aspirations.
- **What's valued** — what a market will actually pay for or promote.
- **What energizes you** — work you'd do without being watched.
The durable career sits in the overlap. Outside it: burnout, mediocrity, or hobby.
Career capital (rare, valuable skill) compounds; "follow your passion" rarely does —
mastery usually produces passion, not the reverse (So Good They Can't Ignore You).
When stuck, prototype: run small reversible experiments (side project, contract,
informational interview) instead of agonizing (Designing Your Life).

## 2. Positioning — specific value to a specific audience

Positioning is NOT a job title. It is: *the specific value you offer to a specific
audience, better than the obvious alternatives.* Write one sentence:
"I help [audience] achieve [outcome] through [your edge]." If it could describe a
thousand people, it positions nothing. Niche down until defensible, then let
reputation widen it. The market rewards being *the* person for a problem, not *a*
person in the category.

## 3. Narrative — the through-line of your trajectory

A career is read as a story. Make past moves look intentional in hindsight: each step
built a capability or set up the next. State the arc — where you came from, the thread
connecting your moves, where you're going and why now. Frame pivots as accumulation
("I bring X to Y"), not wandering. One coherent thread beats a list of jobs.

## 4. Resume / portfolio — impact and proof, not duties

- **Resume = quantified impact, not responsibilities.** Every bullet: result first,
  with a number and the so-what. "Cut deploy time 40% (45→27 min), unblocking daily
  releases" — not "responsible for the deploy pipeline." Use action verb + metric +
  outcome. Tailor per role: mirror the job's language, lead with what *that* role
  values. An untailored resume signals you didn't care.
- **Portfolio / proof over claims.** Show the work: shipped projects, writing, code,
  case studies, a talk, numbers. Demonstrated > asserted. Proof beats any adjective.

## 5. Interview — judgment on display

- **STAR stories.** Pre-bank 6–8 concrete stories (Situation, Task, Action, Result,
  with numbers) covering conflict, failure, leadership, ambiguity, impact. Map them
  to likely questions. Specifics beat generalities every time.
- **Research.** Know the company, the role, the people, recent news. Generic answers
  read as low interest.
- **Why-you / why-them.** Have a crisp, true answer to both. This is positioning
  applied to one role.
- **Ask questions that show judgment.** "How do you decide what *not* to build?" reads
  as senior. The interview is two-way evaluation, not an interrogation of you.

## 6. Leverage — build it before you need it

Leverage = **options + reputation + a scarce, valued skill.** It is built in advance,
not summoned in a crisis. Cultivate a credible alternative (side income, a standing
offer, savings) *before* you need to walk or ask. Reputation and rare skill are the
slow compounders — invest while employed and unpressured. You negotiate, transition,
and say no from strength only when you built the option earlier.

## 7. Network — give first, weak ties

Relationships are built before they're needed, by giving (intros, help, useful signal)
without a scoreboard. **Weak ties** — acquaintances, not your inner circle — surface
most opportunities, because they reach networks you can't see. Networking only when
job-hunting is transactional and reads as such. Keep relationships warm continuously.

## 8. Evaluate an offer / transition — total package, not salary

Judge a role on: **total comp** (base + bonus + equity + benefits, modeled honestly),
**growth** (will you build career capital? who will you learn from?), **people**
(manager and team you'd work with daily), and **optionality** (does it open doors or
close them?). Salary alone is a trap. Weigh against your direction (Section 1) and your
BATNA (staying / other options). For the comp *number and mechanics*, hand off to the
negotiation pack.

## Honesty boundary

This pack sharpens *truthful* positioning, framing, and proof. It will not fabricate
credentials, invent experience, or inflate titles/metrics. Framing real achievement
is leverage; lying is fraud that collapses on contact.
