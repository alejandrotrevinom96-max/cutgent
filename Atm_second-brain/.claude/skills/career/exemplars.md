# Career Exemplars (borrow the mechanism, not the surface)

## 1. Career capital before passion — Steve Martin's "be so good they can't ignore you" (Newport, *So Good They Can't Ignore You*)
Martin spent ~10 years grinding a genuinely novel act before fame arrived. He didn't
"follow a passion"; he built a rare, valuable skill, and the passion and leverage
followed. **Mechanism:** mastery of something the market values is the asset; meaning
and autonomy are bought with it, not the other way around. **Borrow:** when direction
is unclear, pick the path that fastest builds a rare, valued skill, and let interest
deepen as competence does — instead of waiting for certainty.

## 2. Positioning as "the only" — niching to a defensible category (positioning practice; cf. Ries & Trout)
A generalist "marketer" competes with thousands; "I help B2B SaaS startups fix
self-serve onboarding conversion" competes with almost no one and commands a premium.
The second person gets the inbound. **Mechanism:** a narrow, named position makes you
the obvious choice for one problem, which is worth more than being a plausible choice
for many. **Borrow:** write your one-sentence position, then keep narrowing the
audience and outcome until it stops sounding like everyone else.

## 3. Impact-framed resume bullet — result first, duty never (resume craft)
Weak: "Responsible for the data pipeline and reporting." Strong: "Rebuilt the data
pipeline, cutting report latency 6h→15min and eliminating ~10 analyst-hours/week of
manual pulls." Same job, opposite signal. **Mechanism:** the reader scans for outcomes
and scope; a number proves you measure your own impact and shows the magnitude.
**Borrow:** rewrite each bullet as action verb + metric + business outcome; if you
can't find a number, find a before/after or a scale (users, dollars, time, %).

## 4. STAR answer that shows judgment, not just activity (interview craft)
Q: "Tell me about a conflict." Weak: "We disagreed and worked it out." Strong (STAR):
"*Situation* — eng and design deadlocked on a launch scope two weeks out. *Task* — I
owned the date. *Action* — I ran the data on which cuts hit retention, proposed
shipping the 3 high-impact items and fast-following the rest, and got both leads to
commit. *Result* — shipped on time; the deferred items landed in the next sprint with
no retention dip." **Mechanism:** the structure forces a concrete situation and a
measurable result, and the *Action* reveals how you think. **Borrow:** pre-write
stories so the Action shows judgment and the Result carries a number.

## 5. Leverage built before it was needed — the standing offer (career capital + options)
An engineer kept one warm relationship at a rival firm and an open-source reputation
going while happily employed. When a reorg hit, she had a credible offer in two weeks
and negotiated her own role from strength — because the option already existed.
**Mechanism:** leverage is an inventory you build in calm times and spend in hard ones;
manufactured under pressure, it isn't credible. **Borrow:** while secure, invest in
reputation, a rare skill, runway, and a few warm external relationships so you can
always walk or ask.

## 6. Weak ties open doors — the acquaintance, not the best friend (Granovetter, "The Strength of Weak Ties")
Granovetter found most people landed jobs through acquaintances, not close friends:
your inner circle knows what you already know, while weak ties bridge to information
and openings outside your bubble. **Mechanism:** novel opportunity flows across the
loose, distant edges of a network. **Borrow:** keep a wide, lightly-tended network and
give first (intros, useful signal, help) so the weak ties exist before you need them —
rather than cold-messaging strangers the week you start a job search.
