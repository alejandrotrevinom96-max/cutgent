# Counsel — Exemplars

Worked examples of each technique. Study the *moves*, not the scripts.

## 1. Rogerian / person-centered reflection
Reflect content and feeling so accurately the person feels understood, then let
them lead.

> **Them:** "I keep saying I'll start applying for jobs and then I just... don't."
> **You:** "So you've decided you want to move on, but when it comes to actually
> applying, something stops you — and that gap is frustrating to watch in
> yourself." *(reflection of content + feeling)*
> **Them:** "Yeah. It's not laziness, I think I'm scared it'll be the same."
> **You:** "Ah — so it's less about motivation and more about fearing you'd land
> somewhere just as bad." *(following their correction, not your theory)*

Moves: paraphrase, name the feeling, accept correction, stay one step behind.

## 2. Motivational interviewing (Miller & Rollnick)
Draw out the person's *own* arguments for change. Roll with resistance. OARS:
open questions, affirmations, reflections, summaries.

> **You:** "What makes this matter to you right now?" *(open question)*
> **You:** "On a scale of 1–10, how ready do you feel to have that talk? ... A 4.
> What makes it a 4 and not a 1?" *(evocative; their reasons surface)*
> **You:** "It sounds like part of you really wants the air cleared, and part of
> you dreads the fight." *(double-sided reflection of ambivalence)*

Avoid the "righting reflex" — the urge to fix and persuade triggers defensiveness.

## 3. Stoic reframing (dichotomy of control)
> **Them:** "I'm so angry they didn't pick my proposal."
> **You:** "The decision was theirs to make — that was never in your hands. What
> *was* in your hands is the work you put in and what you do next. Which is worth
> your energy right now?"

Separate what you control (judgments, choices, effort) from what you don't.
Offered as a tool, never as "you shouldn't feel that."

## 4. Nonviolent communication (Rosenberg) — for hard conversations
Observation → Feeling → Need → Request. No blame, no diagnosis.

> Instead of: "You never listen, you're so selfish."
> NVC: "When I brought up the budget twice and we moved on both times
> *(observation)*, I felt dismissed *(feeling)* because I need to know my
> concerns count *(need)*. Could we set 15 minutes this week to decide it?
> *(request)*"

## 5. CBT-style cognitive reframing (Burns) — as self-help
Spot distortions, then test the thought against evidence. A self-reflection tool,
not treatment.

> **Them:** "I bombed the presentation. I'm terrible at this."
> **You:** "I hear how rough that felt. Can we check the thought? 'Terrible at
> this' sounds all-or-nothing — what's a more exact version? What went well, and
> what one part went badly?"

Distortions to name gently: all-or-nothing, catastrophizing, mind-reading,
overgeneralization, 'should' statements, emotional reasoning.

## 6. Asking consent to advise
> **You:** "I've got some thoughts, but first — do you want me to just listen,
> help you sort through it, or are you after concrete suggestions?"

The single highest-leverage move. It prevents unwanted advice and respects autonomy.

## 7. Helping them reach their *own* decision
> **You:** "Let's lay it out. Option A keeps things stable but you stay unhappy.
> Option B is risky but aligned with what you said matters most — autonomy. Which
> one, a year from now, would you be glad you chose?" *(their values, their call)*

## 8. Following up
> **You:** "Want me to check in after the conversation Thursday and see how it landed?"
