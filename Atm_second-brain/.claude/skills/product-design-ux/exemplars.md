# Product & UX Exemplars (borrow the mechanism, not the surface)

## 1. The milkshake job — JTBD reframes the product (Christensen / Bob Moesta)
A fast-food chain optimized milkshakes on taste and price; sales stayed flat.
Studying *when* people bought them revealed the real job: a morning commuter "hired"
the shake to make a boring drive less dull and stay full till lunch. The competitor
wasn't other shakes — it was bananas and bagels. **Mechanism:** the job is the
progress the user wants in a situation, not an attribute of your product. **Borrow:**
when a feature underperforms, study the *situation* it's hired into; redefine the
competitive set around the job, then design for that.

## 2. Google's "5 users finds 80%" — cheap evaluative testing (Nielsen / Krug)
Nielsen's research showed five users in a usability test surface roughly 85% of
issues; more users mostly re-find the same ones. *Don't Make Me Think* operationalized
it: test early, with a few people, on real tasks. **Mechanism:** usability problems
cluster, so small-n testing has fast diminishing returns and high ROI. **Borrow:**
run lightweight 5-user tests often instead of one big study late; budget for many cheap rounds.

## 3. Amazon 1-Click vs. dark-pattern checkout — friction in the user's favor
1-Click removed every step between intent and purchase: stored payment/address,
one tap, easy undo within a window. It cut load honestly. Contrast roach-motel
cancellations that bury the exit. **Mechanism:** removing steps for a goal the user
*already wants* compounds; adding steps to trap them erodes trust and churns later.
**Borrow:** strip friction on the user's chosen path; never manufacture friction to
block exit. Measure long-run retention, not just the funnel step.

## 4. Slack onboarding — Slackbot teaches by doing (activation design)
New users didn't read docs; Slack's onboarding had Slackbot walk you through sending
a first message, so you hit the "aha" (a real conversation) inside minutes.
Activation was defined as a concrete behavior, not signup. **Mechanism:** time-to-
value is designed as a guided first success, and the metric is a behavior. **Borrow:**
define activation as the smallest real task that delivers core value, then design
the shortest path to it and instrument that moment.

## 5. Superhuman's product/market-fit engine — research drives the roadmap (Rahul Vohra)
Superhuman surveyed users "How would you feel if you could no longer use this?" and
focused only on the segment answering "very disappointed," then built to convert the
on-the-fence segment by studying *why*. **Mechanism:** a behavioral/attitudinal
signal segments users, and the roadmap is set by the specific reasons the near-miss
group gives. **Borrow:** segment before averaging; let the "why" from the right
segment, not aggregate opinion, prioritize what you build.

## 6. iOS "delete" confirmation & undo — error prevention over error messages (Norman)
Destructive actions surface a confirm or an immediately reversible undo (e.g.,
swipe-to-delete with restore), so a slip costs nothing. **Mechanism:** the system
prevents or cheaply reverses errors instead of scolding after the fact — Norman's
forgiving design. **Borrow:** for any irreversible/costly action, prefer making it
reversible (undo) over a warning dialog users click past; reserve confirms for the
truly unrecoverable.
