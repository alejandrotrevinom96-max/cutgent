# Cinematography

A binary, shot-by-shot procedure. Generic craft is the floor. **Whenever
`personal/` recall returns the user's real context — their visual brand, a
project's established look, their actual lens/camera kit, references they've
chosen — that OVERRIDES the generic defaults below.** Surface the conflict,
then follow personal/.

Every shot must answer one question first: **what should the audience feel and
where should their eye go?** Geometry, light, and motion are means to that.

## 1. Intent (before any setup)

- **One idea per shot.** State the emotional beat and the single subject the eye
  must land on. If a shot has two jobs, it has none.
- **Subtext drives image.** Power, isolation, intimacy, threat — decide which,
  then pick tools that say it (low angle = power; wide empty frame = isolation).

## 2. Lens & framing

- **Focal length is psychology, not just field of view.** Wide (≤24mm) =
  immersion, distortion, space, unease up close; normal (~35–50mm) = neutral,
  human; long (85mm+) = compression, flattened depth, voyeur/isolation, soft
  background. Move the camera to change perspective; change the lens to change
  feeling.
- **Compose deliberately.** Place the subject on a third or use deliberate
  central symmetry — never accidental dead-center. Keep correct **headroom** and
  **lead/look room** in the direction of gaze or motion. Use leading lines,
  foreground layers, and frames-within-frames for depth.
- **Aperture sets depth of field with intent.** Shallow to isolate, deep to tie
  subject to environment. Don't shoot wide-open by reflex.

## 3. Exposure & dynamic range

- **Expose for the face/subject and protect highlights.** Know your camera's
  latitude; meter the brightest detail you must keep. Shoot flat/log only if you
  will actually grade. Set a deliberate contrast intent (crushed and moody vs.
  open and naturalistic) — don't let it happen by accident.

## 4. Lighting

- **Key, fill, ratio.** Set key direction and quality first; fill controls the
  **key:fill ratio** (high ratio = dramatic/contrasty; low = soft/flat). Add
  back/rim only to separate subject from background.
- **Quality and direction carry mood.** Hard light (small/distant source) =
  edge, tension; soft (large/close, diffused) = flattering, calm. Side/top/under
  light each say something — choose, don't default to frontal.
- **Motivate it.** Every light should plausibly come from a source in the world
  (window, lamp, sky). Motivated light reads true; unmotivated light reads "lit."

## 5. Movement

- **Move only with reason.** Push in to intensify/reveal; pull out to isolate or
  release; track to follow intent; handheld for unease/immediacy; locked-off for
  stillness/control. A move that doesn't change meaning is a distraction — lock it.

## 6. Coverage, continuity, blocking

- **Block actors first, then put the camera where the scene wants it.** Staging
  is composition in time.
- **Pick a line of action and keep the camera on one side (180° rule)** so
  screen direction and eyelines stay consistent across cuts; cross it only
  on-camera or with intent. Match **eyelines** in singles.
- **Shoot coverage that cuts:** establishing/wide, mediums, singles/OTS,
  inserts; vary size and angle enough that adjacent shots aren't jump cuts.
- **Rhythm.** Hold for the beat the moment needs; cut on motion/action. The cut
  is part of the image — shoot so it has somewhere to go.

## 7. Color & through-line

- **Decide palette and contrast intent** for the scene and keep it consistent.
  Color should encode story (warm/cold, saturated/muted), not decorate.

## Honesty boundary

This pack sharpens *your* shot choices, framing, and plan. It will not invent
specs for a camera/lens it can't verify, claim a look is achievable without the
right light or grade, or pretend a rule is unbreakable — every guideline here is
broken on purpose by good DPs once they know why it exists. When gear or
feasibility is uncertain, say so and test before the shoot day.
