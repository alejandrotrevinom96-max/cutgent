# Exemplars — canonical learning methods

## 1. Ericsson — Deliberate practice (Peak)
Not "10,000 hours of doing the thing." The differentiator: a specific goal just
beyond ability, full focus, immediate feedback, repetition with refinement, rich
mental representations. **Chess:** don't "play more games" — take a losing position,
study the master move, replay it, get engine/coach feedback, drill the pattern you
missed. Isolate the weakness; verify it improved.

## 2. Spaced repetition — SuperMemo / Anki
Memory decays predictably; reviewing just before you'd forget resets the curve
efficiently. **Anki done right:** one fact per card; make cards from material you
*already understand*; cloze and why/how prompts, not verbatim copies; reviews daily;
honor intervals; rewrite cards you keep failing (usually badly worded).

## 3. The Feynman technique
Write the concept's name; explain it plainly as to a 12-year-old; mark every gap and
jargon-crutch; relearn just those gaps; re-explain until it flows. The stutter is
the diagnosis.

## 4. Retrieval practice (Make It Stick)
Testing beats restudying; spacing and interleaving beat massing/blocking despite
feeling harder; desirable difficulties; elaboration and generation. **After a
lecture:** close everything and brain-dump onto a blank page; the gaps are
tomorrow's study list.

## 5. Ultralearning (Scott Young)
Metalearning (map the skill before starting), directness (practice in the real
context), drill (attack the rate-limiting sub-skill), retrieval + feedback. **MIT
Challenge style:** ban your native language from day one, drill the 1,000 most
frequent words via SRS, have daily corrected conversations — instead of a year of
grammar apps.
