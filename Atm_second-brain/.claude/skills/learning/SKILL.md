# Learning

A method for learning things so they stick. It replaces the default loop — reread,
highlight, feel familiar, forget — with the loop the evidence supports: retrieve,
struggle, get feedback, space it out, connect it, encode it.

## Personal layer overrides this

Before applying anything below, recall the user's `personal/` context: their goal,
real deadline, why they want this, what they've tried, and known weak points.
**Personal context wins over generic advice.** If they've abandoned Anki three
times, don't prescribe Anki as step one — fix the friction first. Say so when you
override.

## The loop

1. **Define the skill and success criteria.** What, concretely, will you be able to
   *do*? Write the test of done. No measurable target → no learning project.
2. **Decompose into sub-skills.** Break it into separately practicable components;
   find the bottleneck sub-skill gating the rest and attack it.
3. **Deliberate practice.** Work at the *edge of ability* with **immediate
   feedback** and focused correction. Comfortable repetition isn't practice.
4. **Active recall, not review.** Close the book and retrieve (self-test, blank-page
   recall, problems, flashcards). The effort of retrieval *is* the learning;
   rereading/highlighting mostly isn't.
5. **Space it out.** Review at expanding intervals (1d, 3d, 7d, 21d). A little
   forgetting before each review is what makes it durable. Use an SRS for facts.
6. **Interleave.** Mix problem types/topics instead of blocking one until "mastered."
   It feels worse and produces better transfer.
7. **Feynman / teach it back.** Explain in plain language to a beginner; where you
   stall or reach for jargon is exactly what you don't understand — relearn, repeat.
8. **Elaborate and connect.** Ask *why* and *how*; link to what you know and to
   concrete examples. Isolated facts fade; connected ones stick.
9. **Encode as evergreen notes.** Write atomic, concept-titled notes in your own
   words, link them, turn testable atoms into SRS cards. (Follow vault-conventions
   for formatting — don't duplicate it.) What you can't write clearly, you haven't
   learned.

## Defaults

- A project with a real deliverable and deadline beats open-ended "studying."
- Generation before presentation: try to answer before you read the solution.
- Difficulty is the signal — if practice feels easy, raise the bar or interleave.
- One bottleneck sub-skill at a time. Finish the encode step; unencoded learning leaks.
