# Negotiation Exemplars (borrow the mechanism, not the surface)

## 1. Camp David Accords — interests beneath positions (Fisher & Ury, *Getting to Yes*)
Egypt and Israel held incompatible *positions* over the Sinai (each wanted the
territory). The deadlock broke when negotiators dug to *interests*: Egypt wanted
sovereignty, Israel wanted security. A demilitarized Sinai under Egyptian flag
satisfied both. **Mechanism:** reframe from positions to interests; the win is
often on a different axis than the fight. **Live use:** when stuck on one number,
ask "why does this matter to you" and look for an unshared interest to trade on.

## 2. Voss / FBI — tactical empathy and calibrated questions (Voss, *Never Split the Difference*)
In high-stakes hostage talks, demanding or bargaining failed; *labeling* emotions
("It seems like you're worried about being blamed") and asking "How am I supposed
to do that?" lowered tension and extracted information and concessions. **Mechanism:**
empathy is information-gathering and de-escalation, not weakness; questions shift
the problem-solving burden to the other side. **Borrow:** honest labeling + open
questions, never scripted manipulation.

## 3. Anchoring studies — first-number gravity (Tversky & Kahneman; Galinsky & Mussweiler)
Across experiments, the first plausible number stated pulls the final outcome
toward it, even among experts. Aggressive-but-justifiable openers consistently
yield better deals than passive ones. **Mechanism:** the anchor sets the
reference frame. **Borrow:** open first when informed, anchor at the edge of the
defensible range with a rationale; counter extreme anchors by re-anchoring on
facts rather than splitting the difference.

## 4. Orange dispute — value from differing valuations (classic integrative case)
Two parties fight over one orange and "split it." One only wanted the peel (zest
for baking), the other only the juice. Splitting destroyed value both could have
had in full. **Mechanism:** uncovering *why* each wants the thing reveals
non-competing interests. **Borrow:** probe underlying needs before dividing; look
for issues each side weighs differently.

## 5. MESOs — learning preferences without losing ground (Malhotra & Bazerman, *Negotiation Genius*)
Presenting multiple equivalent simultaneous offers (e.g., three packages of equal
value to you, different mixes of price/terms/timeline) reveals which trade-offs
the counterparty values most, signals flexibility, and anchors a range — without
conceding. **Mechanism:** you learn their priorities from which package they lean
toward. **Borrow:** package issues; offer a small menu instead of one take-it number.

## 6. Improving your BATNA — leverage is built, not found (Fisher & Ury)
Negotiators who line up a credible second buyer/supplier *before* the conversation
get materially better terms — the deal's gravity shifts to their walk-away. **Mechanism:**
leverage comes from your alternative, and alternatives are cultivated in advance.
**Borrow:** spend prep effort making the BATNA real, then negotiate calmly because
you can genuinely walk.
