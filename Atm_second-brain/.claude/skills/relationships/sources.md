# Sources

## Canonical (core method)
- John & Julie Gottman — *The Seven Principles for Making Marriage Work*; *What
  Makes Love Last?*; *The Science of Trust*. Four Horsemen (criticism, contempt,
  defensiveness, stonewalling), soft startup, repair attempts, turning toward bids,
  the ~5:1 positive-to-negative ratio, flooding. Based on longitudinal observational
  research; contempt as the strongest predictor of dissolution.
- Sue Johnson — *Hold Me Tight*; Emotionally Focused Therapy (EFT). Attachment-based
  view: identify the negative cycle (pursue-withdraw), externalize it as the shared
  enemy, surface the softer attachment fears underneath. EFT has solid RCT support.
- Marshall Rosenberg — *Nonviolent Communication*. Observation vs. evaluation,
  feelings, universal needs, and requests (not demands): "I feel X when Y because I
  need Z."
- Stone, Patton & Heen (Harvard Negotiation Project) — *Difficult Conversations*.
  The three conversations (what happened / feelings / identity), impact vs. intent,
  the contribution (not blame) frame.
- Brené Brown — *Atlas of the Heart*; *Daring Greatly*. Vulnerability, shame, and
  the "clear is kind" boundary stance.

## Attachment & repair research
- Bowlby & Ainsworth — attachment theory; the Strange Situation. Foundational, well
  supported; the adult-romantic extension (secure/anxious/avoidant) is a useful lens
  but a simplification — treat styles as tendencies on a spectrum, not fixed types.
- Ed Tronick — the Still Face experiments; rupture-and-repair as the normal,
  health-building rhythm of close relationships (repair matters more than never
  rupturing).
- Self-Determination Theory (Deci & Ryan) — autonomy, competence, relatedness as
  core relational needs.

## Note on freshness and evidence quality
Gottman's observational findings and EFT's clinical trials are comparatively
well-supported; many popular relationship claims (specific ratios applied literally,
rigid "attachment style" typing, love-languages framing) are simplifications or have
mixed empirical support — present them as useful heuristics, not laws, and flag
uncertainty. None of this is a substitute for a licensed couples/family therapist;
for abuse, safety, or clinical concerns refer out. The `personal/` layer (the user's
own relationships, history, and what has actually worked for them), pulled via
recall, supersedes everything here when present.

## Safety / referral resources
- Imminent danger: local emergency number (US 911).
- US National Domestic Violence Hotline: call 1-800-799-7233, or text START to
  88788 (thehotline.org). Surface the local equivalent for the user's region.
- For clinical or entrenched issues: a licensed couples/family therapist (e.g.
  AAMFT, or an EFT/Gottman-trained clinician).
