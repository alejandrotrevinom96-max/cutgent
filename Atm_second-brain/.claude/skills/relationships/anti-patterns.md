# Relationships Anti-Patterns (detectable failure modes + fix)

## 1. Criticism (attacking character, not the issue) — First Horseman
**Tell:** "You always...", "You never...", "What's wrong with you?" The complaint
targets who they *are* rather than what *happened*.
**Fix:** swap for a specific complaint plus a need: "When X happened, I felt Y, and I
need Z." One situation, one feeling, one request — no global character verdict.

## 2. Contempt (superiority, mockery, disgust) — Second Horseman
**Tell:** eye-rolling, sarcasm, name-calling, "you're pathetic," talking down. This
is the strongest single predictor of relationship breakdown.
**Fix:** stop and reset; you cannot problem-solve from contempt. Rebuild a culture of
appreciation and respect — deliberately notice and voice what you value in them.

## 3. Defensiveness (counter-attack / victim stance) — Third Horseman
**Tell:** "It's not my fault, *you're* the one who..."; meeting a complaint with an
excuse or a counter-complaint; zero ownership.
**Fix:** accept even 5% of responsibility out loud ("you're right that I forgot").
Taking some part lowers the other person's guard and is itself a repair attempt.

## 4. Stonewalling (shutting down / walking off) — Fourth Horseman
**Tell:** going silent, monotone, leaving with no plan to return, "I'm fine" through
gritted teeth — usually flooding (physiologically overwhelmed).
**Fix:** name it and take a *real* break: "I'm flooded, I need 20 minutes, I'm not
leaving the conversation." Self-soothe, then come back. Disappearing without a return
is abandonment; a stated, time-boxed pause is self-regulation.

## 5. Mind-reading (asserting their intent as fact)
**Tell:** "You did that on purpose," "I know you don't care," "You're trying to make
me feel bad." Treating an assumption about their inner state as established truth.
**Fix:** state the observable behavior and your story about it, then *ask*: "When you
left without saying bye, I told myself you were angry at me. Were you?" Check, don't
convict.

## 6. Keeping score (the grievance ledger)
**Tell:** "After everything I've done for you," dragging old fights into new ones, a
running tally of who owes whom.
**Fix:** address the current issue on its own; close old ruptures by repairing them
rather than banking them. If a past hurt is unresolved, raise it directly as its own
conversation — don't deploy it as ammunition.

## 7. Ultimatums dressed as "boundaries"
**Tell:** "My boundary is you can't talk to your friend anymore," "If you loved me
you'd...". A demand to control *their* behavior, relabeled "boundary," often as a
threat.
**Fix:** a real boundary governs only *your* action ("if this continues I'll step
away"), and you state it calmly and follow through. If you actually need *them* to
change, make it an honest request and negotiate it — don't coerce.

## 8. Problem-solving before validating (the fix-it leap)
**Tell:** jumping straight to solutions, "just do X," or your rebuttal, while they're
still upset and don't feel heard. Escalation despite a "reasonable" suggestion.
**Fix:** validate first — reflect their feeling and say why it makes sense — and only
then, with consent, move to solutions. Most of the time people need to feel
understood before they can think.

## 9. Self-erasure / fawning (peace at the cost of your needs)
**Tell:** apologizing for everything, taking 100% of the blame, never raising your
own needs, agreeing just to end the discomfort.
**Fix:** own your real share *and* keep your needs on the table. Capitulation isn't
repair; it teaches both people that your needs don't count and breeds quiet
resentment. Aim for two people each owning their part.
