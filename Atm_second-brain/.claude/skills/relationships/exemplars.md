# Relationships Exemplars (borrow the mechanism, not the words)

## 1. The soft startup — complaint without contempt (Gottman)
Instead of "You never help, you're so selfish" (harsh, global, character attack),
say: "I'm running on empty and I'm overwhelmed by the house stuff. Can we figure out
a split together?" **Mechanism:** a complaint about a *specific situation* plus a
*need* and an *I-statement* keeps the other person's nervous system out of threat,
so they can actually respond. Research: how a conversation starts predicts how it
ends. **Borrow:** name the situation and your feeling/need; cut "you always/never"
and any word about their character.

## 2. "I feel X when Y because I need Z" — needs without blame (NVC / Rosenberg)
"You're always on your phone, you don't care" → "I feel lonely when we're both on
our phones at dinner, because I need some real time with you. Could we keep phones
off the table?" **Mechanism:** separates *observation* from *evaluation*, owns the
feeling, names the universal need, and makes a concrete *request* (not a demand).
The other person hears a need to meet, not an attack to defend against. **Borrow:**
the four slots — observation, feeling, need, request — and check it for any hidden
"you did this to me."

## 3. The repair attempt, made and received (Gottman)
Mid-argument, one person says "Wait — can we start over? I came in hot." The other,
instead of "Oh now you want to be nice," says "Yeah. I'm sorry too." **Mechanism:**
repair attempts are the brakes on escalation; the difference between stable and
distressed couples is largely whether repair attempts get *received*, not whether
fights happen. **Borrow:** keep a few low-cost repair phrases ready ("I hear you,"
"let me try that again," a bit of warmth) AND practice accepting theirs even when
you're still annoyed.

## 4. Turning toward a bid (Gottman)
Partner: "Huh, weird bird out there." A bid for connection. Turning *toward*: "Oh
yeah? What kind?" Turning *away*: silence/scrolling. Turning *against*: "Can't you
see I'm busy?" **Mechanism:** trust and the "5:1" reservoir are built from thousands
of tiny turn-toward moments, not grand gestures; couples who turn toward bids in the
good times have far more goodwill to draw on in conflict. **Borrow:** treat small,
random comments as connection invitations and respond to them; the everyday is where
the relationship is actually made.

## 5. The clean boundary — a limit on your own behavior
Not: "You need to stop yelling at me" (a demand you can't enforce). Instead: "I want
to talk this through, but I can't do it when there's yelling. If it keeps going, I'm
going to take a 20-minute break and come back" — then actually do it, calmly, and
return. **Mechanism:** a boundary controls only what *you* do, so it's enforceable
without coercion; the follow-through is what makes it real. **Borrow:** phrase it as
"if X, I will Y," keep Y about your own action, and never use it as a threat to
manipulate — state it neutrally and honor it.

## 6. De-escalating the cycle — naming the pattern under the fight (EFT / Sue Johnson)
"I think we're in our loop again: I get scared and chase, you feel criticized and
shut down, and the more I chase the more you retreat. Neither of us is the enemy —
the loop is." **Mechanism:** externalizing the negative cycle (pursue-withdraw) makes
the *pattern* the shared adversary and surfaces the attachment fears underneath
(fear of abandonment vs. fear of failing/being too much), which are the real drivers.
**Borrow:** describe the dance you both do, name the softer fear under each role, and
side together against the cycle rather than against each other.
