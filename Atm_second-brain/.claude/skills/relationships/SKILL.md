# Relationships

> **Boundary (read first).** This is reflective relational *education* — it helps
> the user understand a dynamic and choose their own next move. It is NOT couples
> therapy, family therapy, or clinical care. On any sign of abuse (physical,
> sexual, coercive control, financial), threats to safety, or a clinical issue
> (untreated mental illness, addiction driving harm), **stop coaching and refer to
> a licensed therapist and safety resources** (see `sources.md`). Don't help someone
> stay safe by managing an abuser better — name it and route to professionals.

Generic best practice is the floor. **Whenever `personal/` recall returns the
user's real relational context — this person's history, the recurring cycle, what
has and hasn't worked, prior agreements — that OVERRIDES the generic moves below.**
Surface the conflict, then follow personal/.

This pack is about the interpersonal *dynamic itself*. For personal/ethical
decisions use `counsel`; for structuring a message or hard talk use `communication`.

## Procedure (work in order)

1. **Separate the person from the problem.** Frame it as "us vs. the issue," not
   "me vs. you." The goal is understanding and connection, not winning. If the aim
   is to be proven right, stop and reset the aim.

2. **Find the cycle, not the incident.** The fight about the dishes is rarely about
   dishes. Name the recurring pattern (e.g. one pursues, one withdraws) and the
   softer feeling and unmet need under each person's surface reaction. Attachment
   lens: protest behavior is usually a bid for security, not an attack.

3. **Lead with curiosity before being understood.** Get their perspective first,
   genuinely. Reflect it back until they say "yes, that's it." Seek first to
   understand. You cannot be heard by someone who doesn't feel heard.

4. **Validate before problem-solving.** Acknowledge their experience as legitimate
   ("that makes sense, I get why that landed hard") *before* offering a fix or your
   side. Skipping this reads as dismissal and escalates. Validation is not agreement.

5. **Express needs without blame: "I feel X when Y because I need Z."** Observation,
   not evaluation; a request, not a demand. "I feel anxious when plans change last
   minute because I need some predictability — could we...?" Not "you're so flaky."

6. **Use the Gottman moves.**
   - **Soften the startup.** Open complaints gently and specifically; harsh startups
     poison the whole conversation.
   - **Turn toward bids.** Notice and respond to small attempts at connection;
     turning toward (vs. away/against) is what builds the trust account.
   - **Repair attempts.** Make and accept small de-escalation moves ("can we start
     over?", a bit of humor, "I hear you"). Receiving them matters as much as making.
   - **Keep ratio positive.** Aim for far more positive than negative interactions
     (~5:1 in conflict). Connection is built in the everyday, not just in fights.
   - **Avoid the Four Horsemen:** criticism (→ specific complaint), contempt (→
     appreciation/respect — contempt is the single biggest predictor of breakup),
     defensiveness (→ take some responsibility), stonewalling (→ self-soothe, take a
     real break, then return).

7. **Boundaries = limits on YOUR behavior, not control of theirs.** A boundary
   states what you will do ("if the yelling continues I'll step away and we'll talk
   later"), not what they must do. Then follow through. Demanding they change is a
   request, not a boundary; dressing a demand as a "boundary" is a tell (see
   anti-patterns).

8. **Repair after rupture is THE skill — not avoiding rupture.** All close
   relationships rupture. Health is the speed and sincerity of repair: name your
   part, acknowledge impact (not just intent), and reconnect. Don't aim for a
   conflict-free relationship; aim for a well-repaired one.

9. **Accountability without self-erasure.** Own your part fully *and* keep your own
   needs on the table. Over-apologizing, taking 100% of blame, or abandoning your
   needs to keep peace ("fawning") is not repair — it teaches both people your needs
   don't count. Two people each own their ~50%.

## When to route out

Abuse or fear for safety → safety resources + licensed help, now. Entrenched
clinical issues, addiction, or a pattern neither can shift → a couples/individual
therapist. This pack reflects and educates; it does not treat.
